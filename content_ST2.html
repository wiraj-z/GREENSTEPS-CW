SDG 13 - Climate Action

Waste Reduction & Recycling

The waste we generate every day has a far greater impact on the climate than most people realise. From landfill methane to the emissions embedded in producing and discarding goods, waste is a significant driver of climate change. By reducing what we consume and recycling more of what remains, individuals and communities can make a meaningful difference.

01

How Waste Drives Climate Change

Waste is not just an environmental nuisance - it is a significant source of greenhouse-gas emissions. When organic waste decomposes in landfill sites without oxygen, it releases methane, a gas that is over 80 times more potent than carbon dioxide over a 20-year period. At the same time, producing new goods from virgin materials consumes enormous amounts of energy, most of which still comes from fossil fuels.

The global waste sector accounts for around five percent of total greenhouse-gas emissions, but when the full lifecycle of products is considered - extraction, manufacturing, transport, and disposal - the true climate cost of our throwaway culture is far higher. Tackling waste is therefore one of the most accessible and impactful climate actions available to individuals.

Key facts about waste and emissions:

  • Landfill sites are among the largest human-made sources of methane emissions globally.
  • Food waste alone is responsible for roughly eight percent of global greenhouse-gas emissions.
  • Manufacturing new products from raw materials typically produces significantly more emissions than using recycled ones.
  • Single-use plastics contribute to both carbon emissions and long-term pollution of natural ecosystems.

02

The Problem with Landfill

Burying waste in landfill sites is still the most common method of disposal worldwide, but it is also one of the most damaging for the climate. As organic material food scraps, garden waste, paper breaks down in an oxygen-free environment, it generates methane gas. Many landfill sites capture some of this gas, but a significant portion escapes directly into the atmosphere.

Beyond methane, landfill also produces leachate, a toxic liquid that can contaminate soil and groundwater. Hazardous materials such as batteries, electronics, and certain plastics release chemicals that persist in the environment for centuries. Reducing the volume of waste sent to landfill is therefore essential for both climate and ecological health.

Why landfill is a climate problem:

  • Decomposing organic waste produces methane, a highly potent greenhouse gas.
  • Landfill gases contribute to local air pollution as well as global warming.
  • Valuable materials are permanently lost rather than returned to the economy.
  • Expanding landfill sites destroys natural habitats that store carbon.

03

The Climate Power of Recycling

Recycling diverts waste from landfill and replaces the need to extract and process virgin raw materials. Making aluminium from recycled cans uses around 95 percent less energy than producing it from bauxite ore. Recycled paper, glass, and plastics all offer similar - though varied - energy savings compared to their virgin-material equivalents.

When individuals and communities recycle consistently and correctly, the cumulative effect on emissions is substantial. However, contamination of recycling streams - putting the wrong items in recycling bins - can render entire batches unprocessable. Learning what can and cannot be recycled in your local system is one of the most practical steps you can take.

Advantages of effective recycling:

  • Dramatically reduces the energy needed to produce new materials.
  • Keeps valuable resources in use rather than sending them to landfill.
  • Lowers emissions associated with mining, logging, and raw material processing.
  • Creates green jobs in collection, sorting, and reprocessing industries.

04

Reduce and Reuse Come First

Recycling is valuable, but it still requires energy and resources. The most effective strategy is to reduce consumption in the first place, and to reuse items as many times as possible before they reach the end of their life. This is why environmental frameworks place "reduce" and "reuse" above "recycle" in the waste hierarchy.

Simple everyday choices - buying less, choosing durable goods over disposable ones, repairing rather than replacing, and borrowing or sharing items - can significantly shrink the amount of waste a person generates and the emissions embedded in their consumption. Young people are increasingly leading cultural shifts towards repair cafés, second-hand shopping, and zero-waste lifestyles.

How to reduce and reuse in daily life:

  • Carry reusable bags, bottles, and containers to avoid single-use packaging.
  • Buy second-hand clothing, electronics, and furniture before purchasing new.
  • Repair broken items or take them to a local repair café rather than discarding them.
  • Plan meals carefully to reduce food waste, and compost unavoidable scraps.

05

Towards a Circular Economy

The circular economy is a model designed to eliminate waste entirely by keeping materials and products in use for as long as possible. Rather than the traditional "take, make, dispose" approach, a circular economy designs products to be repaired, remanufactured, and ultimately recycled back into new goods with minimal loss of quality or material.

Transitioning to a circular economy could reduce global greenhouse-gas emissions by as much as 39 percent, according to research by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation. Young people will both shape and benefit from this transition. Careers in sustainable product design, materials science, supply chain management, and environmental policy all offer opportunities to directly influence how future economies handle resources.

Emerging circular economy solutions:

  • Products designed from the outset to be disassembled and recycled.
  • Industrial symbiosis, where one company's waste becomes another's raw material.
  • Extended producer responsibility schemes that hold manufacturers accountable for end-of-life disposal.
  • Digital platforms that connect people to repair services, sharing schemes, and second-hand markets.
