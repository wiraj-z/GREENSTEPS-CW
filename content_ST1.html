SDG 13 — Climate Action

Sustainable Transport and Climate Change

Transportation is one of the largest sources of greenhouse-gas emissions worldwide. The choices people make about how they travel every day have a direct impact on the climate. By understanding these effects and choosing lower-emission alternatives, individuals and communities can make a meaningful difference.

01

How Transport Affects the Climate

The transport sector is responsible for approximately one quarter of global carbon dioxide emissions from energy use. Cars, lorries, aeroplanes and ships all burn fossil fuels, releasing greenhouse gases that trap heat in the atmosphere and drive climate change.

In many countries, transport emissions have continued to rise even as other sectors have reduced their output. This makes changing how people travel one of the most important steps in addressing the climate crisis.

Key facts about transport emissions:

  • Road vehicles account for the majority of transport-related CO₂ emissions.
  • Short-haul flights produce a disproportionately high level of emissions per kilometre.
  • Freight transport contributes significantly to global supply chain emissions.
  • Urban areas generate a large share of transport emissions due to high population density and car dependency.

02

The Environmental Cost of Private Vehicles

Private cars are convenient, but they are also one of the least efficient ways to move people. A car carrying only one person produces far more emissions per passenger than a bus or train carrying many travellers.

Beyond direct emissions, private vehicles also contribute to urban congestion, air pollution, and land use for roads and car parks. Reducing dependence on private cars is a key strategy for more sustainable cities.

Why single-occupancy car journeys are costly:

  • Emit more CO₂ per passenger kilometre than any shared transport mode.
  • Contribute to poor air quality through exhaust and tyre particles.
  • Increase urban noise pollution and road congestion.
  • Require large areas of land for parking and road infrastructure.

03

The Climate Benefits of Public Transport

Buses, trains, trams and metro systems can carry large numbers of passengers at a fraction of the emissions produced by the same number of private cars. Well-used public transport is one of the most effective tools for reducing urban carbon footprints.

Switching from a car to a bus or train for regular commutes can significantly reduce an individual's annual transport emissions. When many people make this switch, the cumulative impact on a city or region can be substantial.

Advantages of using public transport:

  • Lower emissions per passenger compared to private vehicles.
  • Reduces total number of vehicles on the road, easing congestion.
  • Electric trains and trams produce near-zero direct emissions.
  • Encourages denser, more walkable urban development.

04

Walking and Cycling as Climate Action

Active travel — walking and cycling — produces zero direct emissions, requires minimal infrastructure compared to roads, and has significant co-benefits for physical and mental health. For short distances, it is both the most sustainable and often the most practical option.

Many cities are investing in cycling infrastructure, pedestrian zones and bike-sharing schemes to make active travel safer and more accessible. Students and young people can both benefit from and advocate for these improvements.

How to make active travel work for you:

  • Plan routes in advance using cycling maps or pedestrian-friendly apps.
  • Use a bike-sharing scheme if you do not own a bicycle.
  • Walk for journeys under 20 minutes instead of taking a vehicle.
  • Combine cycling or walking with public transport for longer trips.

05

The Future of Sustainable Transport

Electric vehicles, hydrogen-powered transport and smart mobility systems represent the next phase of sustainable transport. These technologies can dramatically reduce emissions from road and rail travel when powered by renewable energy.

Young people will both shape and benefit from these developments. Careers in transport engineering, urban planning, energy systems and policy offer opportunities to directly influence how future communities travel.

Emerging sustainable transport solutions:

  • Electric vehicles charged by renewable energy sources.
  • Hydrogen fuel cells for lorries, trains and ferries.
  • Smart traffic systems that reduce idling and congestion.
  • Mobility-as-a-service platforms that reduce private car ownership.
